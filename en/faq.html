FAQ — Structured rhinoplasty

Clear answers about male rhinoplasty, revision cases, and augmentation (Black/Asian noses), including septal and rib cartilage grafting when indicated.

What is “structured rhinoplasty”?

A support-first approach focused on stability and predictability. Instead of only reducing, the plan prioritizes building/maintaining support with techniques and grafts when indicated.

Does “natural” mean “minimal change”?

Not necessarily. Natural means harmonious proportions without obvious surgical signs (over-rotated tip, overly pinched tip, artificial dorsum, or loss of identity).

How is male rhinoplasty different?

Male planning uses different proportions and aesthetic lines. The goal is to preserve masculine features and avoid feminization, with precise control of tip position and dorsum definition.

When is revision rhinoplasty indicated?

When there is aesthetic dissatisfaction, persistent asymmetry, late deformities, retraction, dorsum irregularities, or loss of support after prior surgery. Revision requires careful planning and realistic expectations.

Why do revisions sometimes need rib cartilage?

After previous surgeries, septal cartilage may be limited. Rib cartilage can be indicated when additional material is needed for reconstruction, augmentation, and structural support.

Indications are individualized and discussed during consultation.

Augmentation (Black & Asian noses): what is the focus?

In many cases, the goal is augmentation and definition with stable support, respecting identity and facial proportions. Planning differs from standard reduction-focused rhinoplasty.

How long until the “final result”?

Healing is gradual. Major swelling improves in the first months, but refinement can take many months, especially in thick skin, revision cases, and augmentation/reconstruction.

Is there a guarantee?

No. Surgery involves biological variability and healing response. The commitment is to technique, planning, transparency, and appropriate follow-up—with aligned expectations.

Is an online consult enough to decide?

Online consults are excellent for initial assessment, expectation alignment, and second opinions—especially in revisions. For definitive planning and physical exam, an in-person visit may be needed.
