Revision evaluation: identify what failed, rebuild support, and define a realistic plan

Revision rhinoplasty is among the most technical procedures in facial plastic surgery. It requires a careful analysis of current anatomy, prior changes, scar tissue, and what must be reconstructed. My approach is structured, prioritizing support, function, and natural aesthetics.

When to consider a revision evaluation

  • Tip drop, weak support, or an “unstable” tip
  • Persistent asymmetries that do not improve over time
  • Nostril retraction or sidewall collapse (valve issues)
  • Dorsal irregularities, depression, or “step-offs”
  • An artificial or “operated” appearance
  • Breathing concerns after surgery

Timing is individualized. In many cases, scar maturation must be respected before definitive decisions.

Why revision is different

Anatomy has already been altered, internal scarring is present, and cartilage may be limited. Strategy differs significantly from primary rhinoplasty.

What I evaluate

Tip/dorsum support, symmetry, skin thickness, nasal valves, retraction, history and healing pattern. The goal is understanding the cause, not only the appearance.

How I plan

First: support and function. Then: aesthetic refinement. The most stable outcomes often preserve natural identity and respect anatomical limits.

Common failure patterns (examples)

  • Overresection: too much removed → collapse, depression, irregularities
  • Insufficient tip support → drop, asymmetry, late deformities
  • Alar retraction and lateral wall collapse
  • Dorsal irregularities due to scarring and/or lack of support
  • Nasal valve compromise → breathing difficulty

Similar complaints can have different causes. Diagnosis drives the plan.

Grafts: when do they matter?

In revision surgery, grafts are often needed to rebuild support and contour. When indicated, I may use septal cartilage and, in selected cases, rib cartilage to restore structure and predictability.
